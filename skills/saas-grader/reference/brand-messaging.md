# Brand & Messaging (BM-1 to BM-8)

## BM-1: Productize Your Headline

**What it is:** Frame your software as a specific end-to-end product with a clear outcome, not a generic service or vague capability.

**Recommendation:** Sell a productized offering with a clear outcome, message, and price (e.g. don't sell an "image editor" — sell "AI video wizz: an automated background remover, for $79/year"). People will find it easier to understand the value, be more likely to buy, pay a higher price, and be happier with the outcome.

**Why it matters:** When people find something difficult to visualize, it feels like a riskier purchase, so they are less likely to buy. A clearly defined product is easier to picture and is more likely to meet the expectations of the buyer.

**Key stat:** Productized offerings increase willingness to buy and willingness to pay more.

**Source:** Wirtz, J., Fritze, M. P., Jaakkola, E., Gelbrich, K., & Hartley, N. Service products and productization. *Journal of Business Research* (September 2021). **Universities:** National University of Singapore; University of Cologne; Turku School of Economics, University of Turku; Catholic University Eichstaett-Ingolstadt; University of Queensland.

**PASS criteria:**
- Headline names a concrete outcome or end-to-end product
- Specific about what the software does — easy to visualize the value in under 5 seconds
- Clear what you get for what price

**FAIL criteria:**
- Vague headline like "Transform your business" or "Unlock your potential"
- Generic service description rather than a productized offering
- Requires reading further to understand what the product actually does

## BM-2: Highlight Only Your Top 3 Benefits

**What it is:** Showcase exactly 3 key benefits or features above the fold — not more, not fewer.

**Recommendation:** Showcase your 3 best benefits or features (e.g. Fully customizable, World-class cybersecurity, Guaranteed uptime), not more, not less. You will be more persuasive. The effect follows an n-shaped pattern, with 1 or 2, as well as 4 or 5 (and beyond), being less persuasive than 3. Hold off on describing your other features — keep them for a specific "features" page or section.

**Why it matters:** Using 3 positive claims shows a positive pattern. Once people have identified a pattern, they are more likely to make a decision. Any claims beyond that start to make people skeptical, so they backfire. In the study, 3 claims were 10.4% more persuasive than 4.

**Key stat:** 3 claims were 10.4% more persuasive than 4.

**Source:** Shu, S. B., & Carlson, K. A. When three charms but four alarms: Identifying the optimal number of claims in persuasion settings. *Journal of Marketing* (January 2014). **Universities:** UCLA Anderson School of Management, University of California & McDonough School of Business, Georgetown University.

**PASS criteria:**
- Exactly 3 benefits or key features highlighted prominently above the fold
- Benefits are the strongest differentiators, not generic features
- Other features saved for a dedicated features section

**FAIL criteria:**
- More than 3 benefits listed above the fold (4+ triggers skepticism)
- Fewer than 3 benefits (1-2 doesn't establish a pattern)
- Mix of strong and average features diluting the best ones

## BM-3: Use Present Tense

**What it is:** Copy uses present tense to describe the product and its impact, implying something current, universal, and more certain.

**Recommendation:** Write descriptions in the present tense (e.g. "It's very popular with engineers worldwide") not past tense (e.g. "Since 2019 it has been very popular with engineers worldwide"). Present tense implies something current and more universal, making claims seem more certain and trustworthy.

**Why it matters:** Present tense implies something current and more universal, seeming more certain. Past tense with temporal qualifiers limits the scope and certainty of claims.

**Key stat:** Present tense descriptions are perceived as more universal and trustworthy.

**Source:** Practitioner consensus. Present-tense framing is a widely adopted copywriting principle supported by general findings in linguistic concreteness research, but no single peer-reviewed paper isolates verb tense as an independent variable in SaaS conversion contexts.

**PASS criteria:**
- Descriptions use present tense ("Teams love our platform")
- Claims feel current, universal, and certain

**FAIL criteria:**
- Past tense framing ("Since 2019 it has been popular with engineers")
- Temporal qualifiers that limit the scope of claims

## BM-4: Signal Newness (If Applicable)

**What it is:** If the company is relatively new (<5 years) and innovative, they display their founding date or newness prominently. If established, they don't over-emphasize age.

**Recommendation:** People prefer high-tech or innovative products when they are from new brands (vs established, older competitors). If you are relatively new (<5 years) show this off (e.g. "Established in 2024"), especially if your SaaS is highly innovative (e.g. using AI). If you're not new, avoid mentioning it front and center.

**Why it matters:** People prefer high-tech or innovative products from new brands over established competitors. Newness signals cutting-edge capability.

**Key stat:** New brands are preferred for innovative/high-tech products.

**Source:** Practitioner consensus. The preference for new brands in innovative/high-tech categories is a widely observed market pattern supported by general brand perception research, but no single peer-reviewed paper isolates brand age as an independent variable in SaaS purchase contexts.

**PASS criteria:**
- New brands (<5 years) showcase recency ("Established in 2024", "Launched in 2023")
- Particularly effective for AI/innovative products
- N/A for established brands (>5 years) — skip this item

**FAIL criteria:**
- New, innovative brand hides its founding date
- Established brand unnecessarily highlights its age on the homepage

**N/A when:** Brand is established (>5 years old) and not positioning as cutting-edge/innovative.

## BM-5: Use Simple Language

**What it is:** Product descriptions use simple, everyday language rather than technical jargon or complex marketing terminology.

**Recommendation:** When describing your products and features, use simple language. Unfamiliar or technical language is difficult to understand and requires mental effort (e.g. "easy to use" compared to "frictionless toolstack and workflow integration"). If people don't understand a product description, they're also less inclined to trust it and buy it.

**Why it matters:** Unfamiliar or technical language is difficult to understand and requires mental effort. If people don't understand a product description, they're less inclined to trust it and buy it. Simple language reduces cognitive load and increases persuasion.

**Key stat:** Technical language reduces trust and purchase intent.

**Source:** Oppenheimer, D. M. Consequences of Erudite Vernacular Utilized Irrespective of Necessity: Problems with Using Long Words Needlessly. *Applied Cognitive Psychology* (March 2006). **Universities:** Princeton University.

**PASS criteria:**
- Product descriptions use plain, everyday language
- Benefits are immediately understandable without industry knowledge
- No unexplained jargon or buzzwords

**FAIL criteria:**
- Heavy use of jargon ("frictionless toolstack", "synergistic workflow orchestration")
- Technical terms without explanation
- Copy requires industry expertise to understand

## BM-6: Show Sales or View Numbers

**What it is:** The homepage displays concrete numbers — sales count, user count, or view count — as social proof.

**Recommendation:** Showcase your number of sales (e.g. "Trusted by 35,000+ analysts") to boost people's trust and purchase intentions by up to 53.5%. Purchases are a stronger signal than views, but views are much stronger than nothing. For example, "156 views" increased purchase intentions by 30%. So when you are starting out, display your number of views, then once you cross a few hundred sales switch to displaying your number of purchases.

**Why it matters:** Product views or purchases by others are a signal that people use to make assumptions about the product's quality. If others have checked it out or bought it, it must be good.

**Key stat:** Sales numbers boost purchase intentions by up to 53.5%. View counts increase purchase intentions by 30%.

**Source:** Das, G., Spence, M. T., & Agarwal, J. Social Selling Cues: The Dynamics of Posting Numbers Viewed and Bought on Customers' Purchase Intentions. *International Journal of Research in Marketing* (January 2021). **Universities:** Indian Institute of Management Bangalore; Bond University; Haskayne School of Business, University of Calgary.

**PASS criteria:**
- Specific numbers displayed ("Trusted by 35,000+ analysts", "Used by 10,000+ teams")
- Purchase/user count preferred over view count
- Numbers are prominent and easy to find

**FAIL criteria:**
- No numerical social proof anywhere on the homepage
- Vague social proof without numbers ("Trusted by companies worldwide")
- Numbers buried in footer or hard to find

## BM-7: Display a 4-4.5 Star Rating

**What it is:** The displayed average rating is in the 4-4.5 star range, not a perfect 5.0.

**Recommendation:** Aim to show an average review rating of around 4.5 stars, not a perfect 5-star score. Cultivate this rating on external platforms (e.g. G2, Capterra) and in what you show on your own website. Average review ratings increase sales as they go up and peak at between 4 and 4.5 stars, then slightly lower them again. So while you should aim for an excellent rating (5 stars is better than 3), it's alright to have some negative reviews that lower your average.

**Why it matters:** If the average rating for a product is extremely high, people tend to think that they are too good to be true. They suspect that the company could have manipulated them. Instead, when some negative reviews are present but the rating is still good (4 to 4.5 stars), people assume that the reviews honestly discussed both positive and negative aspects, but the product still came out as great.

**Key stat:** Sales peak at 4-4.5 stars, then slightly decline. A few negative reviews boost your sales if they're irrelevant to most customers.

**Source:** Maslowska, E., Malthouse, E. C., & Bernritter, S. F. Too good to be true: the role of online reviews' features in probability to buy. *International Journal of Advertising* (June 2016). **Universities:** University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign; Northwestern University; King's Business School, King's College London.

**PASS criteria:**
- Average rating displayed is between 4.0 and 4.5 stars
- Rating shown from credible external platforms (G2, Capterra, etc.)
- N/A if no ratings are displayed — note as missing social proof

**FAIL criteria:**
- Perfect 5.0 rating displayed (triggers suspicion)
- Rating below 4.0 displayed prominently
- Only showing cherry-picked 5-star reviews without average

**N/A when:** No ratings are displayed on the site.

## BM-8: Curate the First Testimonial

**What it is:** The first visible testimonial or review highlights core features and unique selling points, not generic praise.

**Recommendation:** When choosing which user testimonials to display first, always opt for ones highlighting the core features and unique selling points of your product. If you have a review section, or on external review websites (e.g. Capterra, G2) do everything you ethically can to receive the first reviews that are positive. You can do so by sharing early access to the product with your loyal existing users (e.g. your beta testers) and offering them extended free access if they leave you an honest review. Most likely, they will leave you a positive one (if they don't, it's a serious signal you need to improve your product).

**Why it matters:** A negative first review leads to a chain reaction of fewer sales, fewer reviews, and more negative reviews. The opposite holds true for seeing a positive review.

**Key stat:** First review sets the tone for all subsequent perception and purchasing behavior.

**Source:** Park, S., Shin, W., & Xie, J. The fateful first consumer review. *Marketing Science* (February 2021). **Universities:** Darla Moore School of Business, University of South Carolina & Warrington College of Business, University of Florida.

**PASS criteria:**
- First testimonial speaks to the product's core value proposition
- Testimonial is from a credible, relevant source
- Positive and specific (not generic praise)

**FAIL criteria:**
- First testimonial is generic ("Great product!")
- First testimonial highlights a minor or irrelevant feature
- No testimonials visible on the homepage
- First testimonial is negative or lukewarm
