# Growth Levers (FT, FM, CP, AF, RF)

## Free Trials

### FT-1: Full-Feature Trial

**What it is:** The free trial grants access to all features with usage limits, rather than restricting which features are available.

**Recommendation:** Offer a high-quality free trial or sample of your product. The free trial should give access to all your features and your content. Instead of limiting the number of features, limit the usage of each (e.g. only 3 downloads during the free trial vs unlimited downloads when paying). Designing your free trial or free samples this way increased revenue by 7.7% in experiments.

**Why it matters:** When a product is high-quality, the sample "taster" creates trust and is the final push needed to make the purchase. Be mindful that for low-quality products, free trials can reduce sales (as people see it's not worth their money).

**Key stat:** Usage-limited (vs feature-limited) trials increased revenue by 7.7%.

**Source:** Li, H., Jain, S., & Kannan, P. K. Optimal design of free samples for digital products and services. *Journal of Marketing Research* (April 2019). **Universities:** The Ohio State University, University of Texas at Dallas, and University of Maryland.

**PASS criteria:**
- Free trial provides access to all (or nearly all) features
- Usage is limited instead of features (e.g., "3 downloads" not "no export feature")
- Trial clearly shows the full product experience

**FAIL criteria:**
- Free trial restricts access to key features
- Only a basic subset available during trial
- Trial experience feels crippled compared to paid
- No free trial offered at all

### FT-2: 7-Day Trial Length

**What it is:** The free trial lasts 7 days, not 14 or 30.

**Recommendation:** Make your free trial last 7 days (instead of 14 or the standard 30). People will be more likely to convert and stay longer, giving you a higher lifetime value (LTV). In a large-scale experiment, 7-day long free trials (vs 30 days) led to 5.6% higher conversions, 6.4% better retention, and 7.9% higher revenue. You could test even shorter trials (e.g. 24 hours, 3 days) as long as they give enough time to actually try the product, but these were not tested in this study.

**Why it matters:** The more intensively people use a product during a free trial the more likely they are to buy it. Shorter trials create a sense of urgency and lead to more intense product use. A lack of urgency of long trials means that people feel less pressure to use the product and are more likely to forget about it.

**Key stat:** 7-day trials: 5.6% higher conversions, 6.4% better retention, 7.9% higher revenue vs 30-day trials.

**Source:** Yoganarasimhan, H., Barzegary, E., & Pani, A. Design and evaluation of optimal free trials. *Management Science* (August 2022). **Universities:** University of Washington.

**PASS criteria:**
- Trial length is 7 days (or shorter if the product can be evaluated quickly)
- Clear urgency communicated about trial timeline

**FAIL criteria:**
- 14-day or 30-day trial (too long, reduces urgency)
- No clear trial length communicated
- Excessively short trial that doesn't allow proper evaluation

### FT-3: Encourage High Trial Usage

**What it is:** The trial experience actively encourages heavy usage through onboarding, recommendations, or engagement features.

**Recommendation:** Focus on encouraging high product usage during the 7-day trial period. Provide a range of features (e.g. grant access to all functionalities the user might need), a comprehensive onboarding flow, dedicated customer support teams, or tailored recommendations to further enhance user experience and involvement. Trials with high usage (30% to 50% above average) lead to more adopters and also reduce any negative word of mouth.

**Why it matters:** Higher usage helps to understand the value of the product and boosts familiarity and trust. High usage during trial periods also creates a sense of commitment, which leads to a higher likelihood of converting to paid.

**Key stat:** Trials with 30-50% above-average usage produce more adopters and reduce negative word of mouth.

**Source:** Foubert, B., & Gijsbrechts, E. Try It, You'll Like It—Or Will You? The Perils of Early Free-Trial Promotions for High-Tech Service Adoption. *Marketing Science* (May 2016). **Universities:** Maastricht University and Tilburg University.

**PASS criteria:**
- Onboarding flow guides new trial users
- Feature recommendations or tips during trial
- Engagement hooks (progress tracking, milestones)

**FAIL criteria:**
- Trial starts with no guidance or onboarding
- No mechanisms to encourage exploration
- Users left to figure out the product alone

**N/A when:** No trial exists.

### FT-4: Extensions Over Discounts

**What it is:** When the trial ends, the company offers trial extensions rather than discounts to encourage conversion.

**Recommendation:** Offer free-trial extensions instead of discounts when trying to convert trial users. A discount makes the price of the product lower, thus seemingly diminishing its value (e.g. people were willing to pay up to 50.1% less for a product when it was offered at a discount rate vs. free). Free trials do not "diminish the value" of the product, and people are more likely to appreciate it.

**Why it matters:** A discount makes the price of the product lower, thus seemingly diminishing its value. People were willing to pay up to 50.1% less for discounted products vs. those offered free. Free trials do not "diminish the value" of the product, and people are more likely to appreciate it.

**Key stat:** Discounts led to 50.1% lower willingness to pay vs. free trial extensions.

**Source:** Palmeira, M. M., & Srivastava, J. Free Offer ≠ Cheap Product: A Selective Accessibility Account on the Valuation of Free Offers. *Journal of Consumer Research* (December 2013). **Universities:** Monash University and Temple University.

**PASS criteria:**
- Trial extension offered as conversion incentive
- Free continued access rather than price reduction

**FAIL criteria:**
- Discount offered immediately at trial end
- No conversion incentive at all
- Only aggressive upgrade prompts without added value

**N/A when:** No trial exists.

## Freemium

### FM-1: Usage-Limited Freemium

**What it is:** The free plan limits usage volume rather than restricting which features are accessible. Bonus: free plan is framed as "$0" rather than "Free."

**Recommendation:** If you use a freemium business model, allow free users to try any of your features (or as many as possible). Instead, limit the usage of each feature at a low level (e.g. only 1 export per month). This is more effective than limiting access to features. In experiments, this approach boosted subscriptions by up to 31%. Frame your freemium as costing $0, rather than "free." Using "$0" or "at no cost" reminds people they are not losing money, rather than gaining something "free." That increases conversions.

**Why it matters:** Being able to sample all types of features allows users to better understand how willing they are to pay for your product. Limiting the access then encourages them to start a paid subscription.

**Key stat:** Usage-limited freemium boosted subscriptions by up to 31%.

**Source:** Aral, S., & Dhillon, P. S. Digital Paywall Design: Implications for Content Demand & Subscriptions. *Management Science* (August 2020). **Universities:** MIT Sloan School of Management.

**PASS criteria:**
- Free users can access all (or most) feature types
- Usage/volume is the limiting factor, not feature access
- Bonus: plan displayed as "$0" or "at no cost" (not just "Free")

**FAIL criteria:**
- Free plan locks out key features entirely
- Users can't experience the core product value on free plan
- Heavy feature gating with minimal free functionality

**N/A when:** No freemium model exists.

---

### FM-2: Freemium Decoy Plan

**What it is:** In a freemium model, there's a third paid plan acting as a decoy to make the main paid plan look like a great deal.

**Recommendation:** In a freemium model, offering only a free plan and one paid plan is suboptimal. You should have a third, decoy, paid plan. The decoy plan should be either: much more expensive than the main plan, with more features and higher usage limits; or slightly cheaper (70%-80% the price of the main plan), with only slightly fewer features or usage limits. In experiments this drew more attention to the main plan, making it seem like a good deal and boosting sales of it between 16% and 23%.

**Why it matters:** People are likely to choose the "middle option", rather than an extreme one (most cheap or most expensive). It seems like a good compromise. People can also experience the attraction effect: seeing a clearly "inferior option" (close in price but quite far from being as good as the main option) makes people more likely to choose the "dominating option."

**Key stat:** Adding a decoy plan boosted main plan sales by 16-23%.

**Source:** Gu, X., Kannan, P. K., & Ma, L. Selling the premium in freemium. *Journal of Marketing* (October 2018). **Universities:** University of Maryland.

**PASS criteria:**
- At least 3 tiers: free, main paid plan, and one more
- The additional paid plan makes the main plan look like better value
- Clear contrast between plans

**FAIL criteria:**
- Only free + one paid plan (no decoy effect)
- All paid plans seem equally balanced (no obvious "best deal")

**N/A when:** No freemium model exists.

## Churn Prevention

### CP-1: Customer Gifts

**What it is:** The company gives unexpected gifts or rewards to long-time customers as a retention strategy.

**Recommendation:** Give free gifts to customers who have been with you long enough. For example, 20% higher extra bandwidth, for free. Make clear the unexpected reward is a thank-you gift for being a loyal customer, with no strings attached. Rewards reduce the churn of long-time customers but also slow down some of their natural increases in spending. Put together, it's still more profitable to reduce their churn. Do not give these to recent customers. This would encourage them to churn and look for more free rewards elsewhere.

**Why it matters:** About 70% of customers are "inert non-switchers" (i.e. they are happy with the status quo and can't be bothered to look for another solution). Free gifts reawaken them to reconsider their options. For customers who have been with you for a while, the free gift is a way to justify and reinforce that they made the right decision in choosing you. This outweighs the likelihood that they will consider other options, and reduces their churn.

**Key stat:** Gifts to loyal customers reduce churn and increase overall profitability.

**Source:** Henderson, C. M., Steinhoff, L., Harmeling, C. M., & Palmatier, R. W. Customer inertia marketing. *Journal of the Academy of Marketing Science* (September 2020). **Universities:** University of Oregon; University of Rostock; Florida State University; University of Washington.

**PASS criteria:**
- Evidence of customer appreciation programs
- Unexpected rewards for loyalty (extra features, capacity, gifts)
- Gifts framed as "thank you" with no strings attached

**FAIL criteria:**
- No visible customer appreciation or loyalty signals
- Only discount-based retention (which can backfire)

**N/A when:** Not observable from the website — note as unverifiable.

### CP-2: Auto-Enrolling Loyalty Program

**What it is:** A simple, automatic loyalty program where customers are enrolled upon subscribing, with clear milestone rewards.

**Recommendation:** Create an automated loyalty program into which new customers are automatically enrolled when they subscribe (e.g. Get 2 months of free when you reach 12 months of subscription). These goals and rewards are clear to the customer in advance. In experiments, this increased projected customer lifetime value by 29.5% over 5 years. 80% of the increase is due to fewer customers leaving, not increased sales.

**Why it matters:** People who are part of a loyalty program feel like the company is investing in their customers. This boosts a sense of connection. People also experience a quest for completeness. For example, if someone needs to stay with the company for a year to unlock a badge, the idea of reaching a milestone could be even more satisfying than the reward itself.

**Key stat:** Auto-enrolling loyalty programs increased projected 5-year CLV by 29.5%. 80% from reduced churn.

**Source:** Gopalakrishnan, A., Jiang, Z., Nevskaya, Y., & Thomadsen, R. Can Non-tiered Customer Loyalty Programs Be Profitable? *Marketing Science* (March 2021). **Universities:** University of Oregon; University of Rostock; Florida State University; University of Washington.

**PASS criteria:**
- Automatic enrollment in loyalty/rewards program
- Clear milestones and rewards communicated upfront
- Simple structure (e.g., "12 months = 2 free months")

**FAIL criteria:**
- No loyalty program visible
- Complex loyalty tiers that are hard to understand
- Opt-in only loyalty programs with poor visibility

**N/A when:** Not observable from the website — note as unverifiable.

### CP-3: Generous Downgrades

**What it is:** The company offers graceful downgrades for customers facing budget constraints, rather than letting them churn entirely.

**Recommendation:** Many customers will churn because of a budget contraction (e.g. lower marketing budget this year and need to cut costs). However, this contraction is often temporary (e.g. in 3 years the budget will be higher than it was before). Do everything you can to detect this type of churn (e.g. questionnaire asking the reason for canceling their subscription) and instead of letting them churn offer an aggressively discounted temporary downgrade. Make sure it's clear that it's temporary (e.g. "To help you adjust to your budget requirements of this year"), and monitor for abuse. When their budget increases again, you will be one of the few subscriptions left in their arsenal, and they will be more likely to increase spending with you, rather than buy from others.

**Why it matters:** When people have to re-evaluate their preferences, they reassess what gives them the most value. If they cut something out, they will likely not return to it. If they stick to it, they will be even happier with it (and self-justify their choice). These updated preferences remain the same even when their budget grows again.

**Key stat:** Downgrade retention preserves customer relationships through temporary budget contractions.

**Source:** Ross, G. R., Meloy, M. G., & Carlson, K. A. Preference Refinement After a Budget Contraction. *Journal of Consumer Research* (June 2020). **Universities:** Texas Christian University; The Pennsylvania State University; College of William and Mary.

**PASS criteria:**
- Downgrade options visible in pricing or cancellation flow
- Temporary discount or lower-tier options for budget-constrained customers
- Clear that downgrade is available as an alternative to cancellation

**FAIL criteria:**
- Only option is full cancellation
- No visible downgrade path
- Downgrade path hidden or difficult to find

**N/A when:** Not observable from the website — note as unverifiable.

## Affiliates

### AF-1: Optimize Affiliate Payment Model

**What it is:** The affiliate payment model is matched to the program type — pay-per-lead for curated programs, pay-per-conversion for open programs.

**Recommendation:** If your affiliate program is highly targeted and curated, where you onboard each affiliate individually and have a close relationship with each, use a pay-per-lead model (a fixed fee for each lead, which you will then nurture and try to convert into a customer). It's more predictable and profitable. If your affiliate model is one-to-many, where the barriers to joining are low (e.g. just sign up and start sharing the affiliate link), use a pay-per-conversion model (affiliates are paid only for who actually converts to a customer). This prevents affiliates from sending fake leads and maximizes affiliate revenue.

**Why it matters:** Pay-per-lead is preferable in one-to-one agreements because it removes the distortion in pricing (i.e. the referrer gets the same payout no matter the spend of the new lead), leading to more efficient outcomes. However, in one-to-many arrangements, pay-per-conversion helps prevent fake leads and aligns both parties' interests by eliminating price distortion.

**Key stat:** Matching the payment model to the affiliate type maximizes revenue and prevents fraud.

**Source:** Libai, B., Biyalogorsky, E., & Gerstner, E. Setting Referral Fees in Affiliate Marketing. *Journal of Service Research* (May 2003). **Universities:** Israel Institute of Technology, University of California.

**PASS criteria:**
- Curated/targeted affiliate program uses pay-per-lead model
- Open affiliate program uses pay-per-conversion model
- Clear affiliate terms and payment structure visible

**FAIL criteria:**
- Mismatched payment model for the affiliate type
- No clear affiliate payment terms
- Pay-per-lead for open programs (incentivizes fake leads)

**N/A when:** No affiliate program exists, or not observable from the website.

### AF-2: Protect From Deceptive Affiliates

**What it is:** The company has safeguards against deceptive affiliate practices including fake engagement, undisclosed partnerships, and brand cannibalization.

**Recommendation:** Require partners and influencers to be transparent about the affiliate partnership. Monitor and identify patterns of fraudulent activities of your affiliates (e.g. overnight spikes in follower counts and disproportionate followers-to-engagement ratios), and eliminate those affiliates from your program. At first glance, it may seem advantageous to keep working with affiliates that are not fully compliant ("it's traffic, after all") but they tend to hurt your brand and rarely lift sales. Set and enforce clear rules when it comes to how affiliates can promote you. For example, affiliates shouldn't be allowed to advertise you directly in search ads (e.g. Google, Bing) if you are doing so yourself — they will cannibalize your efforts.

**Why it matters:** Some influencers inflate engagement metrics using fake accounts or by participating in engagement pods that exchange fake interactions. Higher engagement metrics can lead to better compensation in partnership deals, motivating influencers to manipulate these metrics. Only less than 10% of influencers disclose affiliate links. As people become more aware of the commercial nature of influencer posts, undisclosed affiliations decrease trust and engagement, negatively impacting brand perception and reputation.

**Key stat:** Less than 10% of influencers disclose affiliate links. Undisclosed affiliations decrease trust.

**Source:** Mangiò, F., & Di Domenico, G. All that glitters is not real affiliation: How to handle affiliate marketing programs in the era of falsity. *Business Horizons* (2022). **Universities:** University of Bergamo, Cardiff University.

**PASS criteria:**
- Clear affiliate disclosure requirements visible
- Guidelines for how affiliates can promote the brand
- Monitoring for fraudulent activity mentioned in affiliate terms

**FAIL criteria:**
- No affiliate disclosure requirements
- Affiliates allowed to bid on brand search terms
- No visible affiliate guidelines or terms of service

**N/A when:** No affiliate program exists, or not observable from the website.

## Referrals

### RF-1: Altruistic Referral Framing

**What it is:** Referral program messaging is framed as giving a gift to the friend, not as earning a reward for the referrer.

**Recommendation:** When asking customers to refer you, use an altruistic framing of "Invite a friend to give them a month for free" rather than a selfish "Invite a friend to get a month of free access." In experiments, the likelihood of referrals increased by 60% and was up to 86% more effective. Target your referral campaign to loyal and/or highly satisfied customers (e.g. those who made repeat purchases), and try to ask for a referral right after the customer has purchased your product.

**Why it matters:** Word-of-mouth is by nature something people do altruistically because they think the other person would enjoy it. When there's a selfish financial incentive, the two incentives contrast and damage each other. Instead, describing a referral as something altruistic reduces the senders' guilt of getting a financial reward. It also encourages better targeting by the senders, which leads to higher conversion rates.

**Key stat:** Altruistic framing increased referral likelihood by 60%, up to 86% more effective.

**Source:** Jung, J., Bapna, R., Golden, J. M., & Sun, T. Words Matter! Toward a Prosocial Call-to-Action for Online Referral: Evidence from Two Field Experiments. *Information Systems Research* (May 2020). **Universities:** Temple University; University of Minnesota; University of Southern California.

**PASS criteria:**
- "Give your friend a month free" framing (altruistic)
- Emphasis on benefit to the receiver
- Referral CTA focuses on helping the friend

**FAIL criteria:**
- "Refer a friend and get $20" framing (selfish)
- Emphasis on what the referrer gains
- Transactional language around referrals

**N/A when:** No referral program exists.

### RF-2: Pre-Filled Referral Messages

**What it is:** The referral flow includes pre-filled messages that mention the sender already uses the product.

**Recommendation:** Create workflows to automate and prefill your referral messages. For example, a CTA "invite friend" button pulls up a prefilled email window with the message "I've been using the Grammaroo editor for over 6 months and I love it! I think you'll find it useful too." You can prefill the message in one of two ways, with different effects (don't do both or it will backfire): say that the sender has already bought the product (increases conversions of the receiver by 11%), or explain that there is an incentive for sharing this referral (e.g. "Buy and then share a referral to get a reward") to increase further referrals by 53%.

**Why it matters:** Social learning that a friend has already made a purchase makes people more comfortable in buying a product themselves. Further referrals are motivated by a potential gain and incentive they could then experience themselves.

**Key stat:** Pre-filled messages mentioning sender's purchase increase receiver conversions by 11%. Incentive framing increases further referrals by 53%.

**Source:** Sun, T., Viswanathan, S., & Zheleva, E. Creating social contagion through firm-mediated message design: Evidence from a randomized field experiment. *Management Science* (August 2020). **Universities:** University of Southern California; University of Maryland; University of Illinois.

**PASS criteria:**
- Referral flow auto-populates a message for the sender
- Message mentions the sender's own usage
- Easy one-click sharing

**FAIL criteria:**
- Empty message field requiring user to write from scratch
- No social learning signal in the pre-filled message
- Referral requires manual effort to compose

**N/A when:** No referral program exists.

### RF-3: Avoid Money Rewards

**What it is:** Referral rewards are non-monetary (extra features, free months, partner gifts) rather than cash payments.

**Recommendation:** Avoid using monetary rewards to customers who refer new buyers (e.g. "Invite a friend and get $20"). Instead, offer non-money rewards such as a free extra product (e.g. a free ebook, more data storage). It doesn't have to be one of your own products (e.g. you can offer partner gifts).

**Why it matters:** People recommend products to friends because they want to help them. Money rewards change an altruistic action to a "business transaction." It creates distance and suspicion. Non-money rewards also change social dynamics (the recommender is gaining something instead of just being nice), but the impact is not as drastic as when money is involved.

**Key stat:** Monetary rewards create transactional distance that undermines referral effectiveness.

**Source:** Jin, L., & Huang, Y. When giving money does not work: The differential effects of monetary versus in-kind rewards in referral reward programs. *International Journal of Research in Marketing* (October 2013). **Universities:** Fudan University.

**PASS criteria:**
- Non-monetary rewards (extra features, free months, partner gifts)
- Rewards feel like a genuine bonus, not a transaction

**FAIL criteria:**
- Cash rewards ("Get $20 for each referral")
- Gift cards or direct monetary equivalents as primary reward
- Transactional reward language

**N/A when:** No referral program exists.

### RF-4: Keep Rewards Small

**What it is:** Referral rewards are reasonably small relative to the product value, not disproportionately large.

**Recommendation:** Choose the size of your referral incentives carefully. You don't want them to be too high relative to the value of your product (e.g. a $50 value reward is likely too much for a SaaS that costs $5/month; a referral with a value of $5-$10 would be more reasonable) or in absolute value (even if your SaaS is $500/month, a $1,000 reward is likely too high). Large referral rewards are unprofitable in most cases. They cause a huge boost to your referral rate (+750% in one experiment) but lower the average lifetime value of newly referred customers to a level that in most cases is unprofitable (-48% in the experiment).

**Why it matters:** Referred customers are more valuable than average because people tend to refer people who are an excellent fit for the product. Large rewards motivate to refer beyond that group, to people who are likely not really interested in your product — attracting bad-fit, less committed, price-conscious customers who are less profitable.

**Key stat:** Large rewards: +750% referrals but -48% lifetime value of referred customers (unprofitable).

**Source:** Wolters, H. M., Schulze, C., & Gedenk, K. Referral reward size and new customer profitability. *Marketing Science* (October 2020). **Universities:** University of Hamburg, Frankfurt School of Finance & Management, and Xing.

**PASS criteria:**
- Rewards are proportionate to product value (not excessive)
- Reward value is small relative to subscription cost
- Focus on quality referrals over quantity

**FAIL criteria:**
- Disproportionately large rewards (e.g., $50 for a $5/mo product)
- Rewards that attract bad-fit referrals
- No consideration of reward-to-value ratio

**N/A when:** No referral program exists.

### RF-5: Targeted Referrals

**What it is:** The referral program encourages users to refer people who are likely to be a good fit, not just anyone.

**Recommendation:** Encourage people to only refer friends who are likely to buy your product. For example, remind who it's for (e.g. "Recommend [Product] to other [target users]" or "Invite other bookworms to Goodreads"). If they recommend it to someone who won't follow the recommendation (e.g. because the product is not a good fit for them), the referrer will be up to 26% more likely to churn in the future.

**Why it matters:** People offer recommendations because they think their opinions are sound. When the recommendation is rejected, people's confidence in knowing the best products suffers and they may switch to alternative options to "regain expertise."

**Key stat:** Badly targeted referrals increase the referrer's own churn by up to 26%.

**Source:** Hall, M. J., Hyodo, J. D., & Kristofferson, K. How rejected recommendations shape recommenders' future product intentions. *Journal of Consumer Psychology* (December 2023). **Universities:** Oregon State University, Western University & Ivey Business School.

**PASS criteria:**
- Messaging encourages referring relevant people ("Invite other marketers")
- Clear description of who the product is for
- Guidance on ideal referral targets

**FAIL criteria:**
- No guidance on who to refer (broad, untargeted)
- "Refer anyone!" messaging
- No description of ideal customer profile in referral flow

**N/A when:** No referral program exists.
