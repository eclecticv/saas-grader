# Page Design & Visuals (PD-1 to PD-14)

## PD-1: Design Structure Matches Product Type

**What it is:** The visual design language aligns with what the product promises — structured designs for reliability, unstructured designs for excitement.

**Recommendation:** If people buy your product because it's effective, safe, and reliable (e.g. fintech), use designs that look and feel structured (e.g. geometrical shapes, straight lines, symmetrical layouts). If people buy your product because it is exciting, fun, and enjoyable (e.g. easy-to-use creative tools), use designs that look and feel unstructured (e.g. incomplete shapes, rounded lines, unpredictable layouts). These design principles apply to all your designs (e.g. logo, ads), not only the homepage.

**Why it matters:** Structured designs (e.g. straight lines) feel organized, stable, and predictable. So the brand seems effective and reliable. Unstructured designs (e.g. incomplete shapes) feel surprising, dynamic, and unpredictable. So the brand seems fun and exciting. People constantly look for signals that back up a company's claims. Aligned design elements are a key way to do that.

**Key stat:** Design alignment with brand promise significantly increases brand perception scores.

**Source:** Affonso, F. M., & Janiszewski, C. Marketing by Design: The Influence of Perceptual Structure on Brand Performance. *Journal of Marketing* (November 2022). **Universities:** Oklahoma State University & University of Florida.

**PASS criteria:**
- Reliable/effective products (fintech, security, etc.): geometrical shapes, straight lines, symmetrical layouts
- Fun/exciting products (creative tools, social, etc.): rounded shapes, dynamic layouts, surprising elements
- Design signals match brand promises

**FAIL criteria:**
- Mismatch between product promise and design language
- Reliability-focused product using chaotic, unpredictable design
- Fun/creative product using rigid, corporate-feeling design

## PD-2: Brand-Appropriate Colors

**What it is:** Color palette aligns with the brand's positioning and target audience.

**Recommendation:** Use colors that align with your positioning. Red is associated with excitement (e.g. use for an event ticketing platform), blue with competence (e.g. use for accounting software), and black with sophistication (e.g. use for an engineering project management tool). B2B audiences tend to prefer messages in cold and dark colors (e.g. blue, dark green, purple), as they signal your competence. For B2C audiences, use warm colors (e.g. yellow, bright green, red) to stimulate emotions.

**Why it matters:** Colors act as unconscious signals. B2B audiences prefer cold/dark colors that signal competence. Mismatched colors undermine brand credibility before the visitor reads a single word.

**Key stat:** Color-brand alignment significantly impacts perceived competence and trust.

**Source:** Affonso, F. M., & Janiszewski, C. Marketing by Design: The Influence of Perceptual Structure on Brand Performance. *Journal of Marketing* (November 2022). **Universities:** Oklahoma State University & University of Florida.

**PASS criteria:**
- B2B: cold/dark colors (blue, dark green, purple) signaling competence
- B2C/emotional: warm colors (yellow, red, bright green) stimulating emotions
- Red = excitement, Blue = competence, Black = sophistication
- Colors feel intentional and aligned with brand promise

**FAIL criteria:**
- Color choices contradict the brand positioning (e.g., bright red for accounting software)
- No clear color strategy — random or default palette
- Colors that feel generic or off-brand

## PD-3: Adapt Layout to SaaS Type

**What it is:** The page layout and content structure are adapted based on whether the brand is established, new, or experience-related.

**Recommendation:** If you're an already well-known and trusted brand (e.g. Adobe Photoshop): give more descriptive details, focus on technical specifications, use a comparison matrix, and offer clear recommendations of how to use it (e.g. use cases and scenarios). If your brand is less trusted or not yet well known (e.g. new AI animation generator): focus on entertaining elements (e.g. dynamic visuals, illustrative animations, and GIFs), use interactive elements (e.g. live demos and free trials), and strong social proof (e.g. testimonials or specific case studies). If your SaaS is experience-related (e.g. travel booking system): use conversational language (e.g. "you," "your"), include a lifestyle picture or product video, and make all relevant information instantly available without the need for users to click "show more" buttons.

**Why it matters:** People's decision-making differs for different types of products. Aligning your layouts and designs to that increases sales. The right type of design elements can make your SaaS seem more personalized, user-friendly, and trustworthy.

**Key stat:** Layout alignment with product type increases perceived personalization and conversion.

**Source:** Bleier, A., Harmeling, C. M., & Palmatier, R. W. Creating Effective Online Customer Experiences. *Journal of Marketing* (December 2018). **Universities:** Frankfurt School of Finance & Management, Florida State University, and University of Washington.

**PASS criteria:**
- Trusted brands: detailed specs, comparison matrices, use case recommendations
- New/unknown brands: dynamic visuals, animations, live demos, strong social proof
- Experience-related SaaS: conversational language, lifestyle imagery, all info visible
- Layout strategy matches the brand's market position

**FAIL criteria:**
- New brand relying only on text specs without social proof or demos
- Established brand over-using flashy animations instead of clear documentation
- Experience-related product hiding info behind "show more" buttons

## PD-4: Moderate Page Complexity

**What it is:** The page has a balanced mix of core elements (product description, features) and supporting elements (testimonials, case studies) without being overwhelming.

**Recommendation:** Include a mix of core elements (e.g. description of the main functionalities) and non-core elements (e.g. promotions, testimonials, case studies) to draw more attention and focus to the product. You want the page to look moderately complex, but not too complex (which would overwhelm the viewer).

**Why it matters:** Moderately complex pages draw more attention and focus to the product. Too simple loses engagement; too complex overwhelms and drives visitors away.

**Key stat:** Moderate complexity optimizes attention and focus on the product.

**Source:** Bleier, A., Harmeling, C. M., & Palmatier, R. W. Creating Effective Online Customer Experiences. *Journal of Marketing* (December 2018). **Universities:** Frankfurt School of Finance & Management; Florida State University; University of Washington.

**PASS criteria:**
- Mix of core content (features, pricing) and social elements (testimonials, logos)
- Page feels complete but not overwhelming
- Clear visual hierarchy — easy to scan

**FAIL criteria:**
- Too minimal — bare page with almost no content
- Too cluttered — wall of text, competing elements, no hierarchy
- No supporting elements (testimonials, case studies, trust signals)

## PD-5: Rounded CTA Buttons

**What it is:** Primary CTA buttons use curved edges or oval shapes, not sharp 90-degree corners.

**Recommendation:** Use curved edges or an oval shape (instead of straight angles) on your positive CTA buttons (e.g. "sign up" or "buy"). Across 8 experiments (websites, ads, eye-tracking lab tests) they received 17% to 55% higher click rates and looked at the button 29% longer. For buttons centered on avoidance-related actions (e.g. "Opt-out") or negative framing (e.g. declining an offer), you can use either rounded or straight edges (like 90-degree angles) for your buttons.

**Why it matters:** People prefer round shapes because they associate roundness with friendliness and harmony. They are more likely to approach (click) such friendly, attractive objects. People associate sharp angles with threats and toughness and are less likely to approach (click) them.

**Key stat:** Rounded buttons received 17% to 55% higher click rates and were looked at 29% longer.

**Source:** Biswas, D., Abell, A., & Chacko, R. Curvy Digital Marketing Designs: Virtual Elements with Rounded Shapes Enhance Online Click-Through Rates. *Journal of Consumer Research* (December 2023). **Universities:** University of South Florida; University of Tennessee; Neighborly.

**PASS criteria:**
- Primary CTAs have rounded corners or oval shape
- Clear visual hierarchy — primary CTA stands out
- Rounded buttons used for positive/approach actions

**FAIL criteria:**
- Sharp-cornered rectangle CTA buttons
- No clear CTA button visible above the fold
- CTA blends into page (no visual prominence)

## PD-6: Machine-Written Font

**What it is:** The primary typeface is a clean, machine-written font signaling precision and reliability.

**Recommendation:** Use a machine-written-looking font for your SaaS (e.g. Arial, Roboto, Times New Roman) to appear precise and reliable (unless you're a fun, creative SaaS, in which case you can try using a handwritten-looking one while making sure it's easily readable).

**Why it matters:** Machine-written fonts signal precision and reliability. Handwritten fonts can work for creative/fun products but hurt credibility for professional tools.

**Key stat:** Font style significantly impacts perceived brand precision and reliability.

**Source:** Practitioner consensus. The association between machine-written typefaces and perceived precision/reliability is well-established in branding practice and supported by typeface congruity research. The Biswas et al. (2023) paper originally cited here studied rounded button shapes (PD-5), not font choices.

**PASS criteria:**
- Clean sans-serif or serif machine font for body and headings
- Easily readable at all sizes
- Exception: fun/creative SaaS may use a handwritten-look font if readable

**FAIL criteria:**
- Handwritten-style font for a professional/B2B SaaS
- Font that's hard to read or feels amateurish
- Too many font families creating visual noise

## PD-7: CTA in Upper-Right Quadrant

**What it is:** A CTA button is positioned in the upper-right area of the homepage (typically in or near the navigation bar).

**Recommendation:** Position a CTA button (e.g. "Sign up", "Try it") in the lower part of the upper right quadrant of the homepage. This shouldn't be the only one. You should likely also have CTAs in other places too (e.g. lower on your page). Place over 70% of the essential content (e.g. an explanation about the product, a demo video, a CTA button) above the fold of a diagonal line from the top-left to the bottom-right of the page. Create a clean and minimal design that minimizes distractions and guides the user towards the CTA naturally.

**Why it matters:** People's natural eye movement follows the top-left to the bottom-right diagonal pattern (the Gutenberg pattern). Shaping your homepage to follow this pattern creates a straightforward visual hierarchy, reduces cognitive load, and improves the user experience. Eye-tracking data found people clicked CTA buttons 1-1.5 seconds faster when in the upper-right quadrant.

**Key stat:** CTA buttons clicked 1-1.5 seconds faster when in the upper-right quadrant.

**Source:** Hernandez, A., & Resnick, M. L. Placement of Call to Action Buttons for Higher Website Conversion and Acquisition: An Eye Tracking Study. *Proceedings of The Human Factors And Ergonomics Society* (September 2013). **Universities:** Bentley University.

**PASS criteria:**
- CTA button visible in the upper-right quadrant of the page
- Additional CTAs may exist elsewhere on page
- Clean design that guides the eye naturally to the CTA

**FAIL criteria:**
- No CTA in the upper-right area
- CTA only appears below the fold
- Upper-right area cluttered with competing elements

## PD-8: Before/After Left-to-Right

**What it is:** If the page shows a before/after comparison, the "before" state is on the left and the "after" (improved) state is on the right.

**Recommendation:** Place images that represent the past on the left and images that represent the future on the right (e.g. the result of your solution in action — a disorganized email inbox on the left and organized inbox on the right). People will like your product more. For right-to-left reading cultures (e.g. Hebrew), reverse the positioning with past-related images on the right and future-related images on the left.

**Why it matters:** People naturally associate the past with the left and the future with the right in left-to-right reading cultures, making this layout much more intuitive. Placing an image of the future on the right feels natural and promises a future state (e.g. organized messages). Placing it on the left would intuitively represent the "before."

**Key stat:** Layout alignment with temporal association increases product liking.

**Source:** Chae, B., & Hoegg, J. The Future Looks "Right": Effects of the Horizontal Location of Advertising Images on Product Attribute. *Journal of Consumer Research* (January 2013). **Universities:** University of British Columbia.

**PASS criteria:**
- Before/problem state placed on the left
- After/solution state placed on the right
- N/A if no before/after comparison is shown

**FAIL criteria:**
- After state on the left, before state on the right
- Confusing layout where before/after isn't clear

**N/A when:** No before/after comparison is shown on the page.

## PD-9: Video for Exciting Software

**What it is:** If the software is fun or experiential, the homepage uses video or GIFs to showcase it rather than static images.

**Recommendation:** If your software is fun and enjoyable (e.g. mash-up image generator tool), use plenty of video or GIFs, rather than static pictures, to increase how likely people are to choose it by up to 79%. If your SaaS is particularly functional (e.g. web hosting platform) investing in video is not necessary, unless it's needed to explain complex features. Focus on showing how to use the product and what kind of benefits people will receive by making the purchase.

**Why it matters:** Dynamic formats (e.g. video, slideshow, GIFs) present information in a more lifelike way than static images. This makes it easier to imagine using and enjoying the product and people become more likely to buy it.

**Key stat:** Dynamic formats increase likelihood of choosing fun products by up to 79%.

**Source:** Roggeveen, A. L., Grewal, D., Townsend, C., & Krishnan, R. The impact of dynamic presentation format on consumer preferences for hedonic products and services. *Journal of Marketing* (November 2015). **Universities:** Babson College and University of Miami.

**PASS criteria:**
- Fun/exciting products: video, GIFs, or animations showing the product in action
- Video focuses on usage and benefits, not just corporate messaging
- N/A for purely functional products (hosting, security)

**FAIL criteria:**
- Fun/exciting product relies only on static screenshots
- Video exists but shows corporate talking heads instead of product

**N/A when:** Product is purely functional (e.g., hosting, security, infrastructure).

## PD-10: Video Speed Matches Message

**What it is:** If video is present, its pace matches the message type — slow for quality/benefits, fast for features/price.

**Recommendation:** Use slower video when highlighting product benefits and quality (e.g. "Unlock new levels of creativity with The Creation Pack"). Use faster video when highlighting product features and price (e.g. "The Creation Pack offers a range of video editing tools, now for $65 per month").

**Why it matters:** When something moves slowly people perceive it as further away, so they tend to judge it in more abstract terms and at a higher level (the benefits and the "why" of the product). People judge objects that are closer in more practical, lower-level terms (whether it's actually achievable and "how"). In addition, people are fast at processing characteristics of products (e.g. price, features, realizing that something is a good deal "50% off"), but slower at processing the meaning of them (the benefits, the "why", "I would be happy to use that because...").

**Key stat:** Speed-message alignment increases persuasion effectiveness.

**Source:** Yoon, S., Bang, H., Choi, D., & Kim, K. Slow versus fast: How speed-induced construal affects perceptions of advertising messages. *International Journal of Advertising* (May 2020). **Universities:** Babson College & University of Miami.

**PASS criteria:**
- Benefit-focused messaging: slower, more contemplative video
- Feature/price-focused messaging: faster-paced video
- N/A if no video is present

**FAIL criteria:**
- Fast video paired with quality/benefit messaging
- Slow video paired with feature list or pricing showcase

**N/A when:** No video is present on the page.

## PD-11: Above-the-Fold Prioritization

**What it is:** The most important content — value proposition, primary CTA, and at least one trust signal — is visible without scrolling. The design provides visual cues that more content exists below.

**Recommendation:** Place the value proposition, primary CTA, and at least one trust signal all above the fold. Provide visual cues (cut-off content, arrows, partial elements) indicating more content exists below. Avoid the "false floor" — the above-the-fold area should not look like the entire page.

**Why it matters:** Users spend 57% of their viewing time above the fold and 74% within the first two screenfuls. The 100 pixels just above the fold receive 102% more viewing time than the 100 pixels just below it. Content placed below the fold gets an average of 84% less attention.

**Key stat:** 57% of viewing time is spent above the fold. Content below gets 84% less attention. 75% of users failed to scroll past a full-screen hero section.

**Source:** Nielsen Norman Group. Eyetracking study with 120 participants and 130,000+ fixations across news, ecommerce, blogs, and FAQs. Published on nngroup.com (2018). Also: "The Illusion of Completeness" (NN/g, 2018). **Universities:** Nielsen Norman Group.

**PASS criteria:**
- Value proposition, primary CTA, and a trust signal all visible without scrolling
- Visual cues (cut-off content, arrows, partial elements) indicate more content below
- No "false floor" — the above-the-fold area doesn't look like the entire page

**FAIL criteria:**
- Key conversion content (pricing, social proof, CTA) requires extensive scrolling
- Large hero image or video fills the entire viewport with no scroll cues
- "Illusion of completeness" — the first screen looks like the whole page

## PD-12: Content Succinctness

**What it is:** Page copy is concise and scannable. Information is presented in digestible chunks rather than long prose paragraphs.

**Recommendation:** Write 50% less text than you would for print. Keep paragraphs to 3-4 lines maximum. Present pricing in structured cards/tables, not described in paragraphs. Split information by topic rather than continuous prose.

**Why it matters:** Reading from screens is 25% slower than from print. 79% of users scan pages rather than reading them word-by-word. Only 16% read line by line. Concise, scannable copy is not just a preference — it's how the medium works.

**Key stat:** 79% of users scan rather than read. Reading from screens is 25% slower than print.

**Source:** Nielsen Norman Group. "Be Succinct! Writing for the Web" (nngroup.com, 1997; updated findings). Also: "How Users Read on the Web" (NN/g). **Universities:** Nielsen Norman Group.

**PASS criteria:**
- Copy is concise — no paragraph exceeds 3-4 lines
- Information is split by topic, not presented as continuous prose
- Pricing is shown in structured cards/tables, not described in paragraphs
- Page communicates its message without requiring extensive reading

**FAIL criteria:**
- Walls of text or sales-letter-style prose blocks
- Pricing or features described in paragraph form instead of structured layouts
- Page reads like a brochure, whitepaper, or long-form article
- Same information repeated across multiple sections

## PD-13: Scannable Section Structure

**What it is:** Content is organized into clearly distinct sections with descriptive subheadings that lead with information-carrying words.

**Recommendation:** Chunk content into visually distinct sections with clear boundaries. Use descriptive, specific subheadings (e.g. "Reduce Wasted Ad Spend by 40%") rather than generic labels (e.g. "How It Works"). Lead subheadings with information-carrying words. Support a layer-cake scanning pattern — a user should be able to read only the headings and understand the page.

**Why it matters:** The layer-cake scanning pattern — where users read headings and skip body text — is one of the most effective scanning approaches. Chunked content with descriptive subheadings improves scanning efficiency by 47% compared to dense text blocks.

**Key stat:** Descriptive subheadings improve scanning efficiency by 47%.

**Source:** Nielsen Norman Group. "The Layer-Cake Pattern of Scanning Content on the Web" (nngroup.com). Eyetracking studies documented since 2004. Also: "How Chunking Helps Content Processing" (NN/g). **Universities:** Nielsen Norman Group.

**PASS criteria:**
- Content chunked into visually distinct sections with clear boundaries
- Subheadings are descriptive and specific (e.g., "Reduce Wasted Ad Spend by 40%")
- Subheadings lead with information-carrying words, not generic labels
- Supports layer-cake scanning — a user can read only the headings and understand the page

**FAIL criteria:**
- Long stretches of undifferentiated content with no section breaks
- Generic or vague subheadings ("How It Works", "Why Us", "Our Solution")
- Missing subheadings — body text runs together without structural markers
- Subheadings that start with filler words instead of meaningful terms

## PD-14: Signal-to-Noise Ratio

**What it is:** Every section on the page serves a clear purpose toward the conversion goal. No redundant, decorative, or rarely-needed content competes with high-priority information.

**Recommendation:** Ensure every section has a clear role in the conversion path (establish trust, explain value, prompt action). Remove redundant sections that repeat the same point in different words. Keep supporting content (FAQ, case studies) complementary to core content. Keep page length proportional to the complexity of the product.

**Why it matters:** Users form aesthetic impressions in 50 milliseconds. Every extra unit of information on a page competes with the relevant units and diminishes their relative visibility. More content does not mean more persuasion — it means more noise. Interfaces should not contain information which is irrelevant or rarely needed.

**Key stat:** Aesthetic judgments form in 50ms — roughly 10x faster than reading speed.

**Source:** Nielsen Norman Group. "Aesthetic and Minimalist Design" — Usability Heuristic #8 (nngroup.com). Also: NN/g first-impression research. **Universities:** Nielsen Norman Group.

**PASS criteria:**
- Every section has a clear role in the conversion path (establish trust, explain value, prompt action)
- No redundant sections that repeat the same point in different words
- Supporting content (FAQ, case studies) complements rather than overwhelms core content
- Page length is proportional to the complexity of the product

**FAIL criteria:**
- Redundant sections — same differentiator or claim restated multiple times
- Filler sections that add length without adding conversion value
- 10+ sections on a page for a simple product
- Irrelevant content (company history, mission statements) competing with product value
