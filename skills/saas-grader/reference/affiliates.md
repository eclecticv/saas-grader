# Affiliates (AF-1 to AF-2)

<!-- Built: 2026-03-18 | Based on original peer-reviewed papers cited within each rule -->

## AF-1: Optimize Affiliate Payment Model

**What it is:** The affiliate payment model is matched to the program type — pay-per-lead for curated programs, pay-per-conversion for open programs.

**Recommendation:** If your affiliate program is highly targeted and curated, where you onboard each affiliate individually and have a close relationship with each, use a pay-per-lead model (a fixed fee for each lead, which you will then nurture and try to convert into a customer). It's more predictable and profitable. If your affiliate model is one-to-many, where the barriers to joining are low (e.g. just sign up and start sharing the affiliate link), use a pay-per-conversion model (affiliates are paid only for who actually converts to a customer). This prevents affiliates from sending fake leads and maximizes affiliate revenue.

**Why it matters:** Pay-per-lead is preferable in one-to-one agreements because it removes the distortion in pricing (i.e. the referrer gets the same payout no matter the spend of the new lead), leading to more efficient outcomes. However, in one-to-many arrangements, pay-per-conversion helps prevent fake leads and aligns both parties' interests by eliminating price distortion.

**Key stat:** Matching the payment model to the affiliate type maximizes revenue and prevents fraud.

**Source:** Libai, B., Biyalogorsky, E., & Gerstner, E. Setting Referral Fees in Affiliate Marketing. *Journal of Service Research* (May 2003). **Universities:** Israel Institute of Technology, University of California.

**PASS criteria:**
- Curated/targeted affiliate program uses pay-per-lead model
- Open affiliate program uses pay-per-conversion model
- Clear affiliate terms and payment structure visible

**FAIL criteria:**
- Mismatched payment model for the affiliate type
- No clear affiliate payment terms
- Pay-per-lead for open programs (incentivizes fake leads)

**N/A when:** No affiliate program exists, or not observable from the website.

## AF-2: Protect From Deceptive Affiliates

**What it is:** The company has safeguards against deceptive affiliate practices including fake engagement, undisclosed partnerships, and brand cannibalization.

**Recommendation:** Require partners and influencers to be transparent about the affiliate partnership. Monitor and identify patterns of fraudulent activities of your affiliates (e.g. overnight spikes in follower counts and disproportionate followers-to-engagement ratios), and eliminate those affiliates from your program. At first glance, it may seem advantageous to keep working with affiliates that are not fully compliant ("it's traffic, after all") but they tend to hurt your brand and rarely lift sales. Set and enforce clear rules when it comes to how affiliates can promote you. For example, affiliates shouldn't be allowed to advertise you directly in search ads (e.g. Google, Bing) if you are doing so yourself — they will cannibalize your efforts.

**Why it matters:** Some influencers inflate engagement metrics using fake accounts or by participating in engagement pods that exchange fake interactions. Higher engagement metrics can lead to better compensation in partnership deals, motivating influencers to manipulate these metrics. Only less than 10% of influencers disclose affiliate links. As people become more aware of the commercial nature of influencer posts, undisclosed affiliations decrease trust and engagement, negatively impacting brand perception and reputation.

**Key stat:** Less than 10% of influencers disclose affiliate links. Undisclosed affiliations decrease trust.

**Source:** Mangiò, F., & Di Domenico, G. All that glitters is not real affiliation: How to handle affiliate marketing programs in the era of falsity. *Business Horizons* (2022). **Universities:** University of Bergamo, Cardiff University.

**PASS criteria:**
- Clear affiliate disclosure requirements visible
- Guidelines for how affiliates can promote the brand
- Monitoring for fraudulent activity mentioned in affiliate terms

**FAIL criteria:**
- No affiliate disclosure requirements
- Affiliates allowed to bid on brand search terms
- No visible affiliate guidelines or terms of service

**N/A when:** No affiliate program exists, or not observable from the website.
