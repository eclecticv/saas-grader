# Freemium (FM-1 to FM-2)

<!-- Built: 2026-03-18 | Based on original peer-reviewed papers cited within each rule -->

## FM-1: Usage-Limited Freemium

**What it is:** The free plan limits usage volume rather than restricting which features are accessible. Bonus: free plan is framed as "$0" rather than "Free."

**Recommendation:** If you use a freemium business model, allow free users to try any of your features (or as many as possible). Instead, limit the usage of each feature at a low level (e.g. only 1 export per month). This is more effective than limiting access to features. In experiments, this approach boosted subscriptions by up to 31%. Frame your freemium as costing $0, rather than "free." Using "$0" or "at no cost" reminds people they are not losing money, rather than gaining something "free." That increases conversions.

**Why it matters:** Being able to sample all types of features allows users to better understand how willing they are to pay for your product. Limiting the access then encourages them to start a paid subscription.

**Key stat:** Usage-limited freemium boosted subscriptions by up to 31%.

**Source:** Aral, S., & Dhillon, P. S. Digital Paywall Design: Implications for Content Demand & Subscriptions. *Management Science* (August 2020). **Universities:** MIT Sloan School of Management.

**PASS criteria:**
- Free users can access all (or most) feature types
- Usage/volume is the limiting factor, not feature access
- Bonus: plan displayed as "$0" or "at no cost" (not just "Free")

**FAIL criteria:**
- Free plan locks out key features entirely
- Users can't experience the core product value on free plan
- Heavy feature gating with minimal free functionality

**N/A when:** No freemium model exists.

## FM-2: Freemium Decoy Plan

**What it is:** In a freemium model, there's a third paid plan acting as a decoy to make the main paid plan look like a great deal.

**Recommendation:** In a freemium model, offering only a free plan and one paid plan is suboptimal. You should have a third, decoy, paid plan. The decoy plan should be either: much more expensive than the main plan, with more features and higher usage limits; or slightly cheaper (70%-80% the price of the main plan), with only slightly fewer features or usage limits. In experiments this drew more attention to the main plan, making it seem like a good deal and boosting sales of it between 16% and 23%.

**Why it matters:** People are likely to choose the "middle option", rather than an extreme one (most cheap or most expensive). It seems like a good compromise. People can also experience the attraction effect: seeing a clearly "inferior option" (close in price but quite far from being as good as the main option) makes people more likely to choose the "dominating option."

**Key stat:** Adding a decoy plan boosted main plan sales by 16-23%.

**Source:** Gu, X., Kannan, P. K., & Ma, L. Selling the premium in freemium. *Journal of Marketing* (October 2018). **Universities:** University of Maryland.

**PASS criteria:**
- At least 3 tiers: free, main paid plan, and one more
- The additional paid plan makes the main plan look like better value
- Clear contrast between plans

**FAIL criteria:**
- Only free + one paid plan (no decoy effect)
- All paid plans seem equally balanced (no obvious "best deal")

**N/A when:** No freemium model exists.
