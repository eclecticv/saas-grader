# Free Trials (FT-1 to FT-4)

<!-- Built: 2026-03-18 | Based on original peer-reviewed papers cited within each rule -->

## FT-1: Full-Feature Trial

**What it is:** The free trial grants access to all features with usage limits, rather than restricting which features are available.

**Recommendation:** Offer a high-quality free trial or sample of your product. The free trial should give access to all your features and your content. Instead of limiting the number of features, limit the usage of each (e.g. only 3 downloads during the free trial vs unlimited downloads when paying). Designing your free trial or free samples this way increased revenue by 7.7% in experiments.

**Why it matters:** When a product is high-quality, the sample "taster" creates trust and is the final push needed to make the purchase. Be mindful that for low-quality products, free trials can reduce sales (as people see it's not worth their money).

**Key stat:** Usage-limited (vs feature-limited) trials increased revenue by 7.7%.

**Source:** Li, H., Jain, S., & Kannan, P. K. Optimal design of free samples for digital products and services. *Journal of Marketing Research* (April 2019). **Universities:** The Ohio State University, University of Texas at Dallas, and University of Maryland.

**PASS criteria:**
- Free trial provides access to all (or nearly all) features
- Usage is limited instead of features (e.g., "3 downloads" not "no export feature")
- Trial clearly shows the full product experience

**FAIL criteria:**
- Free trial restricts access to key features
- Only a basic subset available during trial
- Trial experience feels crippled compared to paid
- No free trial offered at all

## FT-2: 7-Day Trial Length

**What it is:** The free trial lasts 7 days, not 14 or 30.

**Recommendation:** Make your free trial last 7 days (instead of 14 or the standard 30). People will be more likely to convert and stay longer, giving you a higher lifetime value (LTV). In a large-scale experiment, 7-day long free trials (vs 30 days) led to 5.6% higher conversions, 6.4% better retention, and 7.9% higher revenue. You could test even shorter trials (e.g. 24 hours, 3 days) as long as they give enough time to actually try the product, but these were not tested in this study.

**Why it matters:** The more intensively people use a product during a free trial the more likely they are to buy it. Shorter trials create a sense of urgency and lead to more intense product use. A lack of urgency of long trials means that people feel less pressure to use the product and are more likely to forget about it.

**Key stat:** 7-day trials: 5.6% higher conversions, 6.4% better retention, 7.9% higher revenue vs 30-day trials.

**Source:** Yoganarasimhan, H., Barzegary, E., & Pani, A. Design and evaluation of optimal free trials. *Management Science* (August 2022). **Universities:** University of Washington.

**PASS criteria:**
- Trial length is 7 days (or shorter if the product can be evaluated quickly)
- Clear urgency communicated about trial timeline

**FAIL criteria:**
- 14-day or 30-day trial (too long, reduces urgency)
- No clear trial length communicated
- Excessively short trial that doesn't allow proper evaluation

## FT-3: Encourage High Trial Usage

**What it is:** The trial experience actively encourages heavy usage through onboarding, recommendations, or engagement features.

**Recommendation:** Focus on encouraging high product usage during the 7-day trial period. Provide a range of features (e.g. grant access to all functionalities the user might need), a comprehensive onboarding flow, dedicated customer support teams, or tailored recommendations to further enhance user experience and involvement. Trials with high usage (30% to 50% above average) lead to more adopters and also reduce any negative word of mouth.

**Why it matters:** Higher usage helps to understand the value of the product and boosts familiarity and trust. High usage during trial periods also creates a sense of commitment, which leads to a higher likelihood of converting to paid.

**Key stat:** Trials with 30-50% above-average usage produce more adopters and reduce negative word of mouth.

**Source:** Foubert, B., & Gijsbrechts, E. Try It, You'll Like It—Or Will You? The Perils of Early Free-Trial Promotions for High-Tech Service Adoption. *Marketing Science* (May 2016). **Universities:** Maastricht University and Tilburg University.

**PASS criteria:**
- Onboarding flow guides new trial users
- Feature recommendations or tips during trial
- Engagement hooks (progress tracking, milestones)

**FAIL criteria:**
- Trial starts with no guidance or onboarding
- No mechanisms to encourage exploration
- Users left to figure out the product alone

**N/A when:** No trial exists.

## FT-4: Extensions Over Discounts

**What it is:** When the trial ends, the company offers trial extensions rather than discounts to encourage conversion.

**Recommendation:** Offer free-trial extensions instead of discounts when trying to convert trial users. A discount makes the price of the product lower, thus seemingly diminishing its value (e.g. people were willing to pay up to 50.1% less for a product when it was offered at a discount rate vs. free). Free trials do not "diminish the value" of the product, and people are more likely to appreciate it.

**Why it matters:** A discount makes the price of the product lower, thus seemingly diminishing its value. People were willing to pay up to 50.1% less for discounted products vs. those offered free. Free trials do not "diminish the value" of the product, and people are more likely to appreciate it.

**Key stat:** Discounts led to 50.1% lower willingness to pay vs. free trial extensions.

**Source:** Palmeira, M. M., & Srivastava, J. Free Offer ≠ Cheap Product: A Selective Accessibility Account on the Valuation of Free Offers. *Journal of Consumer Research* (December 2013). **Universities:** Monash University and Temple University.

**PASS criteria:**
- Trial extension offered as conversion incentive
- Free continued access rather than price reduction

**FAIL criteria:**
- Discount offered immediately at trial end
- No conversion incentive at all
- Only aggressive upgrade prompts without added value

**N/A when:** No trial exists.
