# Churn Prevention (CP-1 to CP-3)

<!-- Built: 2026-03-18 | Based on original peer-reviewed papers cited within each rule -->

## CP-1: Customer Gifts

**What it is:** The company gives unexpected gifts or rewards to long-time customers as a retention strategy.

**Recommendation:** Give free gifts to customers who have been with you long enough. For example, 20% higher extra bandwidth, for free. Make clear the unexpected reward is a thank-you gift for being a loyal customer, with no strings attached. Rewards reduce the churn of long-time customers but also slow down some of their natural increases in spending. Put together, it's still more profitable to reduce their churn. Do not give these to recent customers. This would encourage them to churn and look for more free rewards elsewhere.

**Why it matters:** About 70% of customers are "inert non-switchers" (i.e. they are happy with the status quo and can't be bothered to look for another solution). Free gifts reawaken them to reconsider their options. For customers who have been with you for a while, the free gift is a way to justify and reinforce that they made the right decision in choosing you. This outweighs the likelihood that they will consider other options, and reduces their churn.

**Key stat:** Gifts to loyal customers reduce churn and increase overall profitability.

**Source:** Henderson, C. M., Steinhoff, L., Harmeling, C. M., & Palmatier, R. W. Customer inertia marketing. *Journal of the Academy of Marketing Science* (September 2020). **Universities:** University of Oregon; University of Rostock; Florida State University; University of Washington.

**PASS criteria:**
- Evidence of customer appreciation programs
- Unexpected rewards for loyalty (extra features, capacity, gifts)
- Gifts framed as "thank you" with no strings attached

**FAIL criteria:**
- No visible customer appreciation or loyalty signals
- Only discount-based retention (which can backfire)

## CP-2: Auto-Enrolling Loyalty Program

**What it is:** A simple, automatic loyalty program where customers are enrolled upon subscribing, with clear milestone rewards.

**Recommendation:** Create an automated loyalty program into which new customers are automatically enrolled when they subscribe (e.g. Get 2 months of free when you reach 12 months of subscription). These goals and rewards are clear to the customer in advance. In experiments, this increased projected customer lifetime value by 29.5% over 5 years. 80% of the increase is due to fewer customers leaving, not increased sales.

**Why it matters:** People who are part of a loyalty program feel like the company is investing in their customers. This boosts a sense of connection. People also experience a quest for completeness. For example, if someone needs to stay with the company for a year to unlock a badge, the idea of reaching a milestone could be even more satisfying than the reward itself.

**Key stat:** Auto-enrolling loyalty programs increased projected 5-year CLV by 29.5%. 80% from reduced churn.

**Source:** Gopalakrishnan, A., Jiang, Z., Nevskaya, Y., & Thomadsen, R. Can Non-tiered Customer Loyalty Programs Be Profitable? *Marketing Science* (March 2021). **Universities:** University of Oregon; University of Rostock; Florida State University; University of Washington.

**PASS criteria:**
- Automatic enrollment in loyalty/rewards program
- Clear milestones and rewards communicated upfront
- Simple structure (e.g., "12 months = 2 free months")

**FAIL criteria:**
- No loyalty program visible
- Complex loyalty tiers that are hard to understand
- Opt-in only loyalty programs with poor visibility

## CP-3: Generous Downgrades

**What it is:** The company offers graceful downgrades for customers facing budget constraints, rather than letting them churn entirely.

**Recommendation:** Many customers will churn because of a budget contraction (e.g. lower marketing budget this year and need to cut costs). However, this contraction is often temporary (e.g. in 3 years the budget will be higher than it was before). Do everything you can to detect this type of churn (e.g. questionnaire asking the reason for canceling their subscription) and instead of letting them churn offer an aggressively discounted temporary downgrade. Make sure it's clear that it's temporary (e.g. "To help you adjust to your budget requirements of this year"), and monitor for abuse. When their budget increases again, you will be one of the few subscriptions left in their arsenal, and they will be more likely to increase spending with you, rather than buy from others.

**Why it matters:** When people have to re-evaluate their preferences, they reassess what gives them the most value. If they cut something out, they will likely not return to it. If they stick to it, they will be even happier with it (and self-justify their choice). These updated preferences remain the same even when their budget grows again.

**Key stat:** Downgrade retention preserves customer relationships through temporary budget contractions.

**Source:** Ross, G. R., Meloy, M. G., & Carlson, K. A. Preference Refinement After a Budget Contraction. *Journal of Consumer Research* (June 2020). **Universities:** Texas Christian University; The Pennsylvania State University; College of William and Mary.

**PASS criteria:**
- Downgrade options visible in pricing or cancellation flow
- Temporary discount or lower-tier options for budget-constrained customers
- Clear that downgrade is available as an alternative to cancellation

**FAIL criteria:**
- Only option is full cancellation
- No visible downgrade path
- Downgrade path hidden or difficult to find
